# 15. Red Team Your Blueprint

## Adversarial Red Team Assessment: BlackReef Affiliate View

**Role:** BlackReef Ransomware Lead Affiliate

**Target:** MedDefense Health Systems (Post-FY26 Security Roadmap Deployment)

### Part 1: The Attacker's Perspective

#### 1. Viable Kill Chain Analysis: **Kill Chain #4 (BEC and Identity Theft / Session Hijacking)**

* **Why it remains viable:** While Controls 1–6 hardened the perimeter, internal segmentation, and offsite backups, **Kill Chain #4 (BEC)** remains operational. The primary MFA control relies on standard push notifications / TOTP tokens rather than FIDO2/WebAuthn hardware keys.
* **Exploitation:** As an affiliate, I don't need to break network encryption if I can steal a valid session. By deploying an AiTM (Adversary-in-the-Middle) phishing proxy (e.g., Evilginx2) targeting a billing clerk or executive assistant via email, I can bypass standard MFA by capturing the session cookie directly. Because the budget excluded specialized Email Security/DLP and 24/7 Managed SOC (Control 7), the stolen token remains valid long enough to execute financial fraud or internal phishing without triggering real-time off-hours SOC revocation.

#### 2. Alternative Attack Path: Bypassing Implemented Controls (4-Step Sequence)

Because MedDefense's $120k budget **excluded 24/7 MSSP SOC Monitoring (Control 7)** and **Dedicated Medical NAC (Control 8)**, the defense relies on internal Wazuh SIEM rules and Sophos Intercept X. Here is how I exploit those specific deferred gaps:

```text
[Step 1: Phish Clinical Staff via AiTM Proxy]
                  │
                  ▼
[Step 2: Authenticate to Clinical Workstation during Peak Business Hours]
                  │
                  ▼
[Step 3: Exploit Local Legacy Device / Shared Credentials (VLAN 30)]
                  │
                  ▼
[Step 4: Exfiltrate PHI via Low-and-Slow Encrypted Egress]

```

* **Step 1 (Initial Access - AiTM Session Capture):** Send a targeted HR/payroll update phishing link to a nurse station terminal. The user authenticates through my AiTM proxy; I capture the authenticated session cookie and Azure AD token, bypassing Control 2 (MFA).
* **Step 2 (Foothold & Living-off-the-Land):** Using legitimate user credentials, I access a clinical workstation in `VLAN 20` during peak day-shift hours. I avoid deploying off-the-shelf Cobalt Strike binaries that Sophos Intercept X (Control 5) would detect; instead, I execute native administrative commands (`vssadmin`, PowerShell `Get-ADUser`, native `certutil`) to blend into normal log volume.
* **Step 3 (Pivot via Deferred Control Gaps):** Because MedDefense **rejected Control 8 (Dedicated Medical NAC)**, there is no dynamic, identity-based device profiling on the medical subnet (`VLAN 30`). I scan for legacy endpoints that cannot run EDR agents (such as the unpatched Windows XP MRI host or unprotected legacy diagnostic devices). I execute local credential harvesting from memory or exploit known local vulnerability paths on those unmanaged medical assets to establish a secondary, persistent backdoor inside `VLAN 30`.
* **Step 4 (Exfiltration & Extortion without SOC Intervention):** Because MedDefense **rejected Control 7 (24/7 Managed SOC)**, log events generated by Wazuh SIEM (Control 3) rely on internal IT staff reviewing alerts during standard 8 AM–5 PM business hours. I perform "low-and-slow" exfiltration of patient records over standard HTTPS (Port 443) at 2:00 AM on a Saturday. By the time the internal team opens their SIEM dashboard on Monday morning, 40,000 PHI records have already been staged and exfiltrated to my C2 storage server.

#### 3. Dangerous Insider Threat Scenario: **Scenario 2 (Ghost Contractor / Delayed Offboarding)**

* **Why it remains critical:** Despite AUP policies and unique account controls, offboarding remains an **administrative/operational process** rather than a fully automated HR-to-AD API integration. If a third-party vendor or contractor is terminated on a Friday afternoon and IT is not notified until Monday, the contractor's Active Directory account and VPN access remain active over the weekend.
* **Impact:** The insider uses their still-valid, MFA-registered personal device to log into the Westside Clinic VPN, query PACS imaging data or database views, and copy sensitive records without triggering anomalous credential alerts, functioning entirely within "authorized" permissions.

### Part 2: The Honest Assessment

#### 1. Overall Residual Risk Rating: **MEDIUM**

> **Justification:** The primary plan successfully downgraded MedDefense's risk posture from **Critical/High** to **Medium**. The implementation of VLAN segmentation, MFA, EDR, immutable offsite backups, and branch firewalls completely eliminated the "easy button" catastrophic failure modes (e.g., domain-wide automated ransomware encryption and backup destruction). However, the posture cannot be rated "Low" because the organization lacks **24/7 real-time monitoring (SOC)** and **automated DLP/email security**, leaving a persistent window of vulnerability for off-hours data exfiltration and credential phishing.

#### 2. The Single Biggest Remaining Gap

* **Gap:** **Lack of 24/7 Real-Time Security Operations Center (SOC) Response Capability.**
* **Impact:** Deploying Wazuh SIEM (Control 3) gathers logs, but logs alone do not stop an adversary at 2:00 AM. An alert generated on a Friday night sits unaddressed for over 48 hours until internal IT reports on Monday morning. In healthcare extortion, a 48-hour dwell window is more than enough time for an attacker to exfiltrate patient databases.

#### 3. Priority #1 Recommendation for Next Year's Budget (FY27)

* **Recommendation:** **Fund a Co-Managed 24/7 Security Operations Center (MDR / MSSP Integration).**
* **Target Allocation:** **$60,000 – $80,000 / year** (Negotiate a co-managed/tiered MDR service focused on off-hours triage for existing Wazuh SIEM and Sophos EDR alerts).
* **Strategic Value:** Connects existing detective controls (SIEM and EDR) to active, round-the-clock containment responses, effectively closing the off-hours exfiltration window exploited in this red team analysis.
